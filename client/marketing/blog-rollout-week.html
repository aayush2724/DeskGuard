What a DeskGuard install week actually looks like

Riya Nair · April 2026 · 8 min read

We say DeskGuard goes from signed contract to live floor map in about a week. People are usually skeptical of that timeline, so here's exactly what happened during a recent two-floor install at a university library in the Midlands.

Monday: the survey

Two of us arrived at 9am with the building's floor plans already marked up from a remote walkthrough the week before. We spent the morning confirming sensor placement against the actual furniture layout — floor plans are reliably about three years out of date, and this library had added a row of standing desks that weren't on the drawings.

By lunch we had a final sensor map: 14 units across two floors, covering 96 desks. Power was available near most positions; for the four that weren't, we used battery units with a six-month replacement cycle.

Tuesday–Wednesday: installation

Mounting took less time than expected — most units clip onto the underside of desks or shelving with no tools beyond a screwdriver. The slower part was the mesh network: each sensor needs to join the network and confirm signal strength to its neighbours, and a couple of units near a concrete stairwell needed repositioning after initial testing showed weak links.

By Wednesday evening, all 14 units were online and reporting raw signal data to our calibration tool.

Thursday: calibration — the part that always takes longer

If there's one step that consistently runs over, it's calibration. The ghost-seat classifier needs to learn what "occupied" looks like in this specific room — different desk materials, different chair types, and even different typical occupant behaviour (a reading room full of postgrads behaves differently to an undergrad open-plan space) all affect the signal pattern.

We walked the floor repeatedly through different scenarios — empty desk, person sitting still, person with a bag but away from desk, person typing — while the model recalibrated. This took most of Thursday rather than the half-day we'd planned, mostly because one corner of floor 2 had unusually thick concrete pillars that were affecting BLE signal in ways the default model didn't expect.

Friday: dashboard and go-live

With calibration done, Friday was straightforward: we configured the floor map in the dashboard, set up Slack alerts for the facilities team's ghost-seat channel, and put the kiosk display live near the entrance.

By Friday afternoon, students walking past the entrance could see live occupancy for both floors. We left a feedback QR code on the kiosk for the first two weeks — most early feedback was about display brightness, not accuracy, which we took as a good sign.

The honest takeaway

The week went close to plan, but calibration is the step to budget extra time for — especially in older buildings with unusual construction materials. Everything else is genuinely about as fast as it sounds.
