How we built occupancy sensing without cameras

Tobias Mehl · March 2026 · 7 min read

Every investor meeting in our first year included some version of the same question: "Why not just use a camera? It would be cheaper and more accurate."

It's a fair question. Computer vision for occupancy detection is mature, well-documented, and yes — often cheaper per square metre than a sensor mesh. We considered it seriously. We didn't build it, and here's why.

The consent problem doesn't go away

Even with on-device processing and no stored footage, a camera pointed at a study space changes how people use that space. In early conversations with student unions, the reaction to "there will be a camera, but it doesn't record" was consistently negative — the presence of the camera itself was the issue, not what happened to the footage.

For libraries and study halls specifically, this matters more than in, say, a retail environment. Study spaces are used for long, unstructured periods — people read, sleep, eat, take calls, get upset, get distracted. The expectation of being unobserved is part of what makes the space usable.

What signal fusion actually means

DeskGuard's sensor units listen for two passive signal types: BLE advertisements from nearby phones, laptops, and wearables, and WiFi probe requests from devices searching for known networks. Neither requires any app or pairing — it's the same background radio chatter your phone emits constantly.

On its own, either signal is noisy. BLE signal strength varies with phone orientation and battery level; WiFi probe frequency varies by device manufacturer. Fused together, with a model trained on the specific desk geometry, the combination becomes a reliable proxy for "is there a device near this desk, and is it moving in a way consistent with a person being present."

Where the ghost-seat classifier comes in

The fusion model alone tells you "device present." To get to "person present" versus "bag present," we layer in a secondary signal: subtle variations in BLE signal strength caused by a human body's effect on radio propagation. A person sitting at a desk perturbs nearby signals in a detectable, repeatable way that a stationary bag doesn't.

This is the piece that took longest to get right — and the piece that lets DeskGuard tell the difference between "occupied" and "ghost seat" without ever needing to identify who is sitting there.

What we hash, and what we don't keep

Device identifiers (MAC addresses, BLE advertisement IDs) are hashed on the sensor unit itself using a rotating daily salt, and the hashed values are used only to track "is this the same device as 30 seconds ago" for continuity — they're never transmitted off the unit in a form that could be linked back to a specific device over time, and raw signal data never leaves the building.

What reaches the dashboard is a per-desk status: free, occupied, or ghost. That's the entire payload. There's no record of which device, which person, or how long any individual stayed — only aggregate, anonymous desk-level state.

The trade-off, honestly

This approach is harder to build and slightly less accurate in edge cases than vision-based systems — our 94% accuracy figure reflects that trade-off. We think it's the right one for spaces where the expectation of privacy is part of what people are there for.
